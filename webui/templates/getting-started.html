Overview

Distant reading is an approach in literary studies that applies computational methods to literary data, usually derived from large digital libraries, for the purposes of literary history and theory. While the term is collective, and is used to refer to a range of different computational methods of analysing literary data, similar approaches also include macro-analysis, cultural analytics, computational formalism, computational literary studies, quantitative literary studies, and algorithmic literary criticism.

“Distant Reading,” Wikipedia

The Distant Reader is designed to read corpora at scale. It takes text as input, does analysis against it, and outputs sets of structured data called “study carrels.“  Through the use of study carrels, researchers can investigate a range of questions, such as:

  • Who or what is mentioned in this corpus?
  • What do they do?
  • When and where does the action take place?
  • How are they described?
  • How are the manifested over time?
  • What connections can be seen between writers?

Study carrels are useful tools for getting your mind around large corpora because they provide ways to sort, group, and prioritize their content. The key to getting the most out of the Distant Reader is two-fold:

  • Articulate a question you would like to address.
  • Identify a corpus which may be used to answer that question.

The questions can be as rudimentary as "What words are used most frequently in Moby Dick?" or as sublime as "How was love defined during the 19th Century?"

Study carrels can be created from many resource types, including, but not limited to, the following: websites, blogs, social media feeds, or files from your computer. The Reader can analyze just about any type of file including but not limited to: Portable Document Format (.pdf) files, Word (.doc and docx) documents, PowerPoint (.ppt and pptx) files, HTML (.htm and .html) files, plain text (.txt) files, and XML (.xml) files.
