Instructions

The Distant Reader offers several ways to read and analyze large collections of text -- corpora. Read our instructions below to learn how to view previously created corpora ("study carrels") or create own.

  • Overview
  • View Carrels
  • Create Carrels

Overview

Distant reading is an approach in literary studies that applies computational methods to literary data, usually derived from large digital libraries, for the purposes of literary history and theory. While the term is collective, and is used to refer to a range of different computational methods of analysing literary data, similar approaches also include macro-analysis, cultural analytics, computational formalism, computational literary studies, quantitative literary studies, and algorithmic literary criticism.

“Distant Reading” | Wikipedia

The Distant Reader is tool intended to supplement the traditional reading process. Given a set of one or more texts acquired through a variety of means, the Reader: 1) applies different forms of text mining and natural language processing against the texts, 2) saves the results as a set of structured data amenable to computing, 3) summarizes everything in a set of interactive HTML reports, 4) compresses everything into a .zip file, and 5) provides the means for you to view the results online or download the whole thing to your computer. This resulting .zip file is affectionately called a "study carrel".

Input

The Distant Reader can create study carrels from a variety of different inputs, including:

  • A URL
  • A list of URLs
  • A file
  • A .zip file
  • A HathiTrust collection file
  • A bioRxiv metadata (.xml) file
  • A subset of 33,000 items from Project Gutenberg
  • The set of more than 500,000 scholarly journals articles on the topic of COVID-19

Output

The Distant Reader outputs data sets known as study carrels, and they include:

  • A cache of the originally submitted content
  • A set of plain text files transformed from the cache
  • A set of tab-delimited files enumerating the features of the submitted content, and those features include: ngrams, parts-of-speech, named entities, statistically significant keywords, authors, titles, dates, extents, and narrative summaries.
  • All of the above features reduced into an SQLite database file
  • A short rudimentary summary of the whole
  • A set of interactive HTML reports elaborating on the summary
  • A .zip file containing everything above

More about study carrels

Study carrels are computer platform- and network-independent data sets. This means any computer will be able to make use of the files in a study carrel, and study carrels do not require an Internet connection in order to be functional. Study carrels are designed to be useful for decades to come, and you could be located under a mountain, cut off from the Internet, and they ought work just fine.

Study carrels are designed to be used by people as well as computers. Many of the files making up a study carrel are narrative in nature, meaning, they are are intended to be read by people just like yourself. Many other files are designed to be read using spreadsheet applications, database applications, analysis applications, visualizers, concordances, machine learning tools (like topic modelers or classification systems), etc. These files can be read using GUI applications (such as Wordle, AntConc, OpenRefine, or Topic Modeling Tool), via things like Jupyter Notebooks, or from the command line. Reading the narrative texts will provide the student, researcher, or scholar with certain level of understanding -- an understanding akin to the understand one gets through of descriptive statistics. But more complex research questions and deeper understanding can be garnered when the structured data of study carrels is exploited.

Anybody may freely browse and download carrels from the publicly available collection -- the library. As of this writing, the collection includes just more than 2,000 items. Alternatively, we hope you will create your own study carrels. To do so you will first need to authenticate. (We use a system called ORCID for this purpose.) You will then either: 1) submit to the Reader some input in the form of a URL, a list of URLs, a file, a zip file containing many files, a HathiTrust collection file, or a bioRxiv metadata file, or 2) search our indexes and submit the results to the Reader. Once you have submitted a request the Reader will do its good work, keep you informed along the way, and when it is done offer you the opportunity to view the results online or download them to your computer.

Depending on the type, number, and size of the submitted files, the study carrel creation process can take as short as a few minutes to as long as many hours. Based on our experience, a study carrel comprised of a few dozen journal articles will process in about fifteen minutes, and a carrel made of up of a few dozen novels will take about an hour.

Create Carrels

You may create carrels from a variety of data sources.

1

Articulate a research question

Questions can range from the mundane to the sublime. For example, you might ask yourself, "How difficult is it to read a given item?" or "What are some definitions of love, and how do St. Augustine and Jean-Jacques Rousseau compare to those definitions?"

2

Identify a set of textual content that may be able to answer the question

The content needed to answer your question may exist as a set of PDF files on your computer, located at the other end of one or more URLs, be the result of a search against a set of books, etc. Whatever the answer, map the content to one of the eight different types of input the Reader can accept: 1) a URL, 2) a list of URLs, 3) a file, 4) a set of files compressed into a single zip file, 5) a bioRxiv metadata (xml) file, 6) a HathiTrust collection file, 7) the results of a search against Project Gutenberg, or 8) the result of a search against a set of scholarly articles on the topic of COVID-19.

If your content does not map to one of these inputs, which is quite possible, contact us and we'll see what we can do. Software is never done, and if it were, then it would be called "hardware".

3

Assemble your content, and give it a name

In other words, do one of two things: 1) create a list of one or more URLs, zip (compress) a number of files into a zip file, or download the HathiTrust collection file or bioRxiv metadata file, or 2) search the our local indexes of Project Gutenberg content or COVID-related articles. You then need to articulate a name for your content, and this name will be the content's unique identifier. Any name will do as long at it only contains letters (a-zA-Z), numbers (0-9), dashes ("-"), or underscores ("_").

4

Submit the content

If your content is not the result of a search against Gutenberg or COVID articles, create a new carrel and use the resulting page to specify your input. If your content is the result of a search, then refine your query to your satisfaction, and queue the results for processing. In either case, you will eventually be asked to authenticate (sign-in) so we can notify you when processing is complete.

5

Wait patiently

Depending on the type of input provided, the number of files, and the size of the files, processing can take as little as two minutes to many hours to complete. A dozen scholarly journal articles usually processes in less than five minutes. A dozen books takes about a thirty minutes. No matter what, the Reader will send you three email messages: 1) processing has started, 2) processing is half way finished, and 3) processing has finished.

Again, please be patient. If you had to read a dozen books, then how long would it take you? 😉

6

Address your research question

The result of the Distant Reader process is the creation of a downloadable data set affectionally known as a "study carrel". Download the carrel (study-carrel.zip), uncompress it, and use your Web browser to open the file named "index.htm". Alternatively, open index.htm from the Distant Reader website. In either case, use the resulting set of HTML pages and try to answer your research question. How many items are in the collection, and what are their characteristics? If you were to describe the collection in a sentence or two, then what might those sentences be? To what degree are the words in your research question listed in the carrel and in what context?

7

Evaluate

Ask yourself many different types of questions.

8

Iterate

The process of learning is never done; go to Step #1.
